From English Grammar to Spicy Grinding

"If a task is worth doing, it must follow a perfect structure." This was the principle that guided Jessie Jane’s 27-year career as an educator in Andhra Pradesh.

Serving as a Senior School Assistant and Secondary Teacher—ranging from classes in Rajahmundry Rural mandal to classrooms in Gokavaram and Seetanagaram mandals, including ZP High School Mallavaram—she taught English with absolute rigor. In her schoolrooms, mistakes were corrected, rules were respected, and structure was key.

When she moved to Austin, she brought that exact same standard into her kitchen. Finding that store-bought imports lacked the true, layered complexities of real Andhra flavors, she set out to recreate them systematically. To her, a recipe is like grammar rules: cut a single corner, and the composition fails.

Meticulous Sourcing & Calibration

A Strict Pedagogy from Farm to Jar

Just like grading papers, Amma establishes clear guidelines for ingredients. There is no guesswork.

From coordinating the sourcing of raw green mangoes and tangy sorrel (Gongura) leaves, to inspecting sacks of Guntur red chillies at local spice markets, every raw material undergoes a check. If it doesn't meet the grade, it is rejected.

The sun-drying intervals, oil-to-chili ratios, and coarse stone-ground grinding times are measured carefully. We do not use high-heat electric blenders that scorch raw oils. Every jar is packaged under strict Austin, Texas guidelines.

The Kitchen Code

Amma's rules for an authentic jar

01

Inspection of Raw Materials

Just like verifying homework, every batch of mangoes, fresh sorrel leaves, and red chillies is hand-inspected. If a mango has a soft spot or a curry leaf isn't crisp, it is rejected immediately.

02

Traditional Timelines

No cutting corners. The sun-drying of red chillies and fenugreek must take place over specific hours to extract maximum aroma. Grinding is done in stone grinders, refusing electric high-heat blenders that scorch spices.

03

Balanced Spice Science

Andhra food is famous for its heat, but Amma insists on balance. Each recipe uses cold-pressed sesame oil to temper the Guntur chillies, creating a satisfying, layered burn rather than single-note spiciness.
