From English Grammar to Spicy Grinding

"If a task is worth doing, it must follow a perfect structure." — the principle that guided Jessie Jane's 27-year career as an educator in Andhra Pradesh.

Serving as a Senior School Assistant across mandals in Rajahmundry Rural, Gokavaram, and Seetanagaram — including ZP High School Mallavaram — she taught English with absolute rigor. In her classrooms, mistakes were corrected, rules were respected, and structure was everything.

When she moved to Austin, she brought that exact same standard into her kitchen. Finding that store-bought imports lacked the true, layered complexity of real Andhra flavors, she set out to recreate them systematically. To her, a recipe is like grammar: cut one corner and the whole composition fails.

The Kitchen Code

Amma's three non-negotiable rules for every authentic jar

01

Inspection of Raw Materials

Every batch of mangoes, fresh sorrel leaves, and red chillies is hand-inspected before anything is chopped or ground. A soft mango or limp curry leaf means the whole batch waits.

02

Traditional Timelines

No shortcuts. Sun-drying of red chillies and fenugreek must run for specific hours to extract peak aroma. Grinding is done in stone grinders — never electric high-heat blenders that scorch spices.

03

Balanced Spice Science

Andhra food is bold, but Amma demands balance. Each recipe uses cold-pressed sesame oil to temper Guntur chillies, building a deep, layered burn — not single-note heat.
